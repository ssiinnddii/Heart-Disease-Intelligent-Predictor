Quick Facts
  • Heart disease is the leading cause of death globally, responsible for an estimated 17.9 million deaths each year.
  • Up to 80% of premature heart disease and strokes are preventable through lifestyle changes.
  • Many heart conditions show no symptoms until serious damage has already occurred.
  • Regular screening and risk assessment can detect problems early when they are most treatable.

Understanding Your Heart Disease Risk

Heart disease risk is influenced by a combination of factors including age, blood pressure, cholesterol, chest pain type, exercise capacity, and more. Our assessment tool analyses these clinical indicators to estimate your personal risk level and shows which factors contribute the most.

Risk Level Predicted Probability What It Means
Low Risk Below 35% Your clinical indicators suggest a lower likelihood of heart disease. Continue maintaining heart-healthy habits.
Medium Risk 35% – 65% Some risk factors are present. Discuss your results with a healthcare provider and consider lifestyle adjustments.
High Risk Above 65% Significant risk factors detected. Consult a healthcare professional promptly for further cardiac evaluation.
Key Risk Factors
Age

Risk increases with age as arteries naturally stiffen over time.

High Blood Pressure

Forces the heart to work harder, damaging arteries over time.

High Cholesterol

Leads to plaque buildup that narrows and blocks arteries.

Chest Pain Type

Certain types of chest pain are strong indicators of heart disease.

Max Heart Rate

Lower maximum heart rate during exercise may indicate reduced cardiac fitness.

ST Depression

Abnormal ECG readings during stress testing signal potential heart issues.

Warning Signs of Heart Disease
  • Chest pain, pressure, or tightness
  • Shortness of breath
  • Pain radiating to arm, neck, or jaw
  • Dizziness or fainting
  • Unusual fatigue or weakness
  • Irregular heartbeat or palpitations
  • Swelling in legs, ankles, or feet
  • Nausea, cold sweats, or indigestion
Heart-Healthy Prevention
Healthy Diet Low sodium, whole grains, fruits, and vegetables
Regular Exercise At least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week
Regular Screening Monitor cholesterol, BP, and blood sugar annually
Manage Stress Practice mindfulness, adequate rest, and work-life balance
